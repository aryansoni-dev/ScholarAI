CHAPTER 1: INTRODUCTION AND PROBLEM STATEMENT
================================================================================

1.1 Introduction
----------------
The rapid expansion of digital learning resources has fundamentally changed the
way students engage with academic content. While access to study material has
never been greater, the ability to process, understand, and retain that material
at scale remains a persistent challenge. Students today deal with hundreds of
pages of textbooks, research papers, lecture notes, and reference documents —
often without adequate time or tools to study them deeply.

Artificial Intelligence (AI), particularly Large Language Models (LLMs), has
emerged as a transformative technology in the education domain. These models can
understand natural language, summarise complex information, generate practice
questions, and explain concepts in ways tailored to the individual learner.
However, most general-purpose AI tools are not designed with students in mind:
they are not integrated with a personal knowledge base, cannot reference the
student's own uploaded materials, and do not provide structured study workflows.

ScholarAI is a web-based application designed to bridge this gap. It provides a
unified platform where students can upload their study documents (PDFs, images,
text files), automatically extract and index the textual content, and then engage
with that content using a conversational AI tutor. The system supports multiple
study modes — including Q&A, Summarization, Quiz generation, and Flashcard
creation — all grounded in the student's own uploaded material.

1.2 Problem Statement
----------------------
Students face several interconnected challenges when studying from digital
materials:

(a) Information Overload: Large volumes of study material make it difficult to
    identify key concepts and retain critical information.

(b) Lack of Interactivity: Static PDFs and documents do not allow students to
    ask questions, test themselves, or request explanations.

(c) Fragmented Tools: Students must switch between multiple applications —
    document readers, note-taking apps, quiz generators, and chatbots — with
    no integration between them.

(d) Generic AI Responses: General-purpose AI assistants do not have access to
    a student's specific materials and therefore provide generic, often
    unreliable answers.

(e) Accessibility: Premium study tools are often expensive and inaccessible to
    students in developing regions.

ScholarAI addresses all of these problems by combining document management,
AI-powered text extraction, and a context-aware study assistant into a single,
cohesive, and freely accessible web application.

CHAPTER 2: PREVIOUS WORK / LITERATURE SURVEY
================================================================================

2.1 AI in Education
--------------------
The integration of Artificial Intelligence in education (AI-Ed) has been an
active research area since the 1980s, with Intelligent Tutoring Systems (ITS)
being among the earliest implementations. Systems like Carnegie Learning's
Cognitive Tutor demonstrated that adaptive, AI-driven instruction could
significantly improve student outcomes (Koedinger & Corbett, 2006).

With the advent of deep learning and transformer-based language models (Vaswani
et al., 2017 — "Attention Is All You Need"), the capabilities of AI in
understanding and generating natural language expanded dramatically. GPT-3
(Brown et al., 2020) and subsequent models demonstrated strong performance on
reading comprehension, summarisation, and question-answering tasks.

2.2 Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)
-----------------------------------------
A key technique for grounding AI responses in specific documents is
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), introduced by Lewis et al. (2020). RAG
combines a retrieval mechanism (fetching relevant text chunks from a document
corpus) with a language model to generate answers that are accurate and grounded
in the provided source material. ScholarAI implements a simplified version of
this pattern by injecting the full extracted text of the user-selected document
directly into the AI prompt context.

2.3 Related Applications
--------------------------
Several commercial and research applications occupy similar problem spaces:

(a) ChatPDF (chatpdf.com): Allows users to upload a PDF and chat with it.
    Limitation: No multi-mode study support, no knowledge base management.

(b) Quizlet AI: AI-powered flashcard and quiz generation.
    Limitation: Not based on user-uploaded arbitrary documents.

(c) Notion AI: Integrated AI assistant within a note-taking workspace.
    Limitation: Not specialised for study workflows; requires manual note input.

(d) Adobe Acrobat AI Assistant: Summarises and answers questions about PDFs.
    Limitation: Requires a premium Adobe subscription; closed ecosystem.

(e) Perplexity AI: AI-powered search and document Q&A.
    Limitation: Not designed for personal knowledge base management.

ScholarAI differentiates itself by combining personal document management,
automated text extraction, multi-mode AI study interactions, and chat history
persistence in a single open-source-friendly stack — at no cost to the student.

2.4 Technology Foundations
---------------------------
(a) Large Language Models: The GPT-4o-mini model (OpenAI, accessed via
    OpenRouter) is used for its strong instruction-following, speed, and low
    cost per token.

(b) Next.js App Router: Chosen for its hybrid server/client rendering
    capabilities, built-in API routes, and excellent developer experience.

(c) Convex: A real-time reactive database chosen for its seamless integration
    with React, type-safe queries, built-in file storage, and real-time
    subscriptions without requiring manual WebSocket management.

(d) Clerk: Industry-standard authentication provider with support for social
    login, JWT-based session management, and pre-built UI components.

CHAPTER 3: MAJOR OBJECTIVES & SCOPE OF PROJECT
================================================================================

3.1 Primary Objectives
------------------------
1. Build a full-stack web application that allows students to manage a personal
   digital knowledge base of study documents.

2. Implement automated text extraction from uploaded documents (PDFs, images,
   text files) to make content queryable by an AI.

3. Develop a conversational AI chat interface that uses the student's own
   document as context, enabling accurate, grounded responses.

4. Provide multiple AI-driven study modes: Q&A, Summarization, Quiz generation,
   and Flashcard generation.

5. Persist all chat sessions and messages to a database so students can review
   past study interactions.

6. Implement a polished, modern UI with a dark-mode aesthetic appropriate for
   long study sessions.

7. Enable copy and edit actions on chat messages to improve study workflow
   efficiency.

3.2 Scope of the Project
-------------------------
IN SCOPE:
- User authentication and account management (via Clerk)
- Document upload (PDF, image, text) with file size support up to 50MB
- Server-side PDF text extraction using native parsing
- AI OCR for image-based documents
- AI chat with document context injection
- Four study modes: Q&A, Summarize, Quiz, Flashcards
- Chat history persistence (Convex database)
- Knowledge base management: list, view, delete documents
- Copy AI response / Edit user message UX features
- Responsive dark-mode UI

OUT OF SCOPE (for current version):
- Mobile native applications (iOS / Android)
- Real-time collaborative study sessions
- Offline mode / Progressive Web App (PWA)
- Support for video or audio lecture files
- Advanced RAG with vector embeddings
- Plagiarism detection
- Learning analytics dashboard

CHAPTER 4: PROBLEM ANALYSIS AND REQUIREMENT SPECIFICATION
================================================================================

4.1 Functional Requirements
-----------------------------
FR-01: The system shall allow users to register and log in using email/password
       or social login providers (Google, GitHub).

FR-02: The system shall allow authenticated users to upload documents in PDF,
       image (PNG, JPG, WEBP), and plain text formats.

FR-03: The system shall automatically extract text from uploaded documents upon
       upload completion.

FR-04: The system shall store extracted text in the database and associate it
       with the corresponding document record.

FR-05: The system shall display a knowledge base view listing all documents
       uploaded by the authenticated user.

FR-06: The system shall allow users to delete documents from their knowledge
       base.

FR-07: The system shall allow users to select a document as context for an AI
       chat session.

FR-08: The system shall support four study modes: Q&A, Summarize, Quiz,
       Flashcards.

FR-09: The system shall send user prompts along with document context to the AI
       and display the response in a chat interface.

FR-10: The system shall persist chat messages to the database.

FR-11: The system shall allow users to copy AI responses to the clipboard.

FR-12: The system shall allow users to edit past user messages by re-loading
       them into the input field.

FR-13: The system shall allow users to start a new chat session from the
       dashboard.

4.2 Non-Functional Requirements
---------------------------------
NFR-01 Performance: The AI response latency shall be under 5 seconds for
       standard-length documents under normal load.

NFR-02 Scalability: The backend (Convex) shall handle concurrent users without
       manual infrastructure management.

NFR-03 Security: All API routes shall be protected; file uploads shall be
       authenticated via Convex Storage pre-signed URLs.

NFR-04 Usability: The UI shall be intuitive for a first-time user without
       requiring a tutorial.

NFR-05 Reliability: Clerk authentication shall have 99.9% uptime (SLA).

NFR-06 Data Privacy: User documents and chat messages shall be scoped
       exclusively to the authenticated user's account.

NFR-07 Maintainability: Code shall be TypeScript-typed throughout for
       correctness and developer ergonomics.

CHAPTER 5: DETAILED DESIGN (MODELING, ERD, AND DFD)
================================================================================

5.1 Architecture Overview
--------------------------
ScholarAI follows a modern three-tier web architecture:

  [Browser Client (React / Next.js)]
          |
          | HTTPS
          v
  [Next.js App Server (API Routes)]
          |
    +-----+------+
    |             |
    v             v
[Convex DB    [OpenRouter
 & Storage]    AI API]
    |
    v
[Clerk Auth]

The Next.js server acts as the orchestration layer: it handles authentication
middleware, file extraction (via Node.js API routes), and proxies AI requests
to OpenRouter. The Convex backend provides real-time reactive data persistence
and file storage. Clerk handles authentication entirely on its own
infrastructure, providing JWTs that the client and Convex use.

5.2 Database Design (ERD)
--------------------------
The Convex database contains four tables:

USERS
- _id (PK), clerkId (unique), email, name, imageUrl, createdAt

DOCUMENTS
- _id (PK), userId (FK → users.clerkId), name, type, size, storageId,
  url, extractedText, status, createdAt, updatedAt
- Status values: "uploading" → "processing" → "ready" | "error"

CHATS
- _id (PK), userId (FK → users.clerkId), documentId (FK → documents._id,
  optional), title, mode, createdAt, updatedAt
- Mode values: "qa" | "summarize" | "quiz" | "flashcards"

MESSAGES
- _id (PK), chatId (FK → chats._id), role ("user" | "assistant"),
  content, createdAt

Relationships:
- A User owns many Documents (1:N)
- A User initiates many Chats (1:N)
- A Document can be referenced in many Chats (1:N, optional)
- A Chat contains many Messages (1:N)

(Full ERD available in: diagrams/erd.md)

5.3 Data Flow Design (DFD)
---------------------------
Three levels of DFD were produced for this system:

LEVEL 0 — CONTEXT DIAGRAM
At the highest level, the ScholarAI system receives inputs from a single human
actor (Student) and interfaces with three external systems:
  - Clerk (authentication)
  - Convex (data and file storage)
  - OpenRouter/GPT-4o-mini (AI inference)
The Student sends login credentials, file uploads, and study queries to
ScholarAI. The system returns authenticated sessions, extracted text, and
AI-generated study material.
(Diagram: diagrams/dfd_level0.md)

LEVEL 1 — MAIN PROCESSES
The system is decomposed into four primary processes:
  P1: Authenticate User — interfaces with Clerk, issues session tokens
  P2: Upload & Extract Document — stores file, extracts text, updates DB
  P3: Manage Knowledge Base — queries and deletes documents from Convex DB
  P4: AI Chat & Study Modes — builds prompts, calls OpenRouter, persists messages
(Diagram: diagrams/dfd_level1.md)

LEVEL 2 — SUB-PROCESSES
Two processes are further decomposed at Level 2:

P2 (Upload & Extract) breaks into 6 steps:
  2.1 Generate Upload URL → 2.2 Upload File to Storage →
  2.3 Save Document Record → 2.4 Send File to /api/extract-text →
  2.5 Parse PDF / OCR Image → 2.6 Update Document with Extracted Text

P4 (AI Chat) breaks into 7 steps:
  4.1 Select Document + Mode → 4.2 Build Prompt with Doc Context →
  4.3 Get/Create Chat Session → 4.4 Call /api/chat →
  4.5 Save User Message → 4.6 Receive & Display Response →
  4.7 Save Assistant Message
(Diagram: diagrams/dfd_level2.md)

5.4 Class Design
-----------------
The system's classes map to four categories:

Data Entities: User, Document, Chat, Message
  (Correspond directly to Convex DB tables)

API Handler Classes: DocumentsAPI, ChatsAPI, ExtractTextAPI, ChatAPI
  (Correspond to Convex mutation/query functions and Next.js API routes)

Page Components: KnowledgeBasePage, DashboardPage
  (React components that orchestrate UI, state, and API calls)

Key relationships:
  - KnowledgeBasePage depends on DocumentsAPI and ExtractTextAPI
  - DashboardPage depends on ChatsAPI, ChatAPI, and DocumentsAPI (read)
  - DocumentsAPI manages Document entities
  - ChatsAPI manages Chat and Message entities
(Full Class Diagram: diagrams/class_diagram.md)

5.5 Sequence Flows
-------------------
Three key sequence flows are documented:

SD1 — User Sign-In:
  Student → Browser (Clerk SignIn UI) → Clerk (credential validation)
  → Clerk (JWT) → Convex (syncUser mutation) → Redirect to /dashboard

SD2 — Document Upload and Extraction:
  Student uploads file → generateUploadUrl() → PUT to Convex Storage
  → saveDocument() (status: processing) → POST FormData to /api/extract-text
  → pdf-parse extracts text → updateDocumentText() (status: ready)

SD3 — AI Chat Q&A:
  Student sends message → createChat() if new session →
  saveMessage(role: "user") → getModePrompt() injects document context →
  POST /api/chat → OpenRouter GPT-4o-mini → response text →
  saveMessage(role: "assistant") → Display in chat UI
(Full Sequence Diagrams: diagrams/sequence_diagram.md)

7.3 Performance Observations
------------------------------
AI Response Latency : 1 - 4 seconds (GPT-4o-mini, OpenRouter)
Document Upload     : ~2 seconds for a 5 MB PDF (Convex Storage)
Text Extraction     : ~1-3 seconds (pdf-parse server-side)
Page Load (cold)    : ~5-6 seconds (Convex reactive query hydration)
Page Load (warm)    : < 300 ms (cached queries)

7.4 Bugs Encountered and Resolved
------------------------------------
BUG-01: Model ID "google/gemini-1.5-flash" returned 400 error from OpenRouter.
  Fix: Migrated to "openai/gpt-4o-mini" (valid OpenRouter model ID).

BUG-02: JSON body size limit (10 MB) blocked large base64-encoded PDF upload.
  Fix: Migrated /api/extract-text to accept multipart/form-data (FormData).
       Updated Clerk middleware to exclude /api/extract-text from matching.

BUG-03: pdf-parse import failed with "Export default doesn't exist" in
  Next.js 15 Turbopack due to ESM/CJS module resolution conflict.
  Fix: Used dynamic async import() with PDFParse named export resolution.

BUG-04: Chat area did not scroll when AI responses were long.
  Fix: Added min-h-0 to ScrollArea flex container; break-words to bubbles.

BUG-05: AI responded "no document provided" even when document was selected.
  Root Cause: Documents uploaded before extraction fix had empty extractedText.
  Fix: Users must re-upload documents; new uploads populate extractedText.

CHAPTER 8: PROJECT LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE SCOPE
================================================================================

8.1 Current Limitations
-------------------------
L-01: PDF Extraction Fragility
  The pdf-parse library has deep compatibility issues with Next.js 15's
  Turbopack build system due to ESM/CJS module conflicts and missing DOM
  polyfills (DOMMatrix, ImageData). This required multiple workarounds and
  remains a maintenance risk. A more robust solution (e.g., a dedicated
  microservice or Cloudflare Worker) would be more reliable in production.

L-02: No Vector Search / True RAG
  The current implementation injects the entire document text (up to 8000
  characters) into the prompt context. For very large documents (hundreds
  of pages), this means only the first portion is analysed. True Retrieval-
  Augmented Generation with vector embeddings (e.g., using Pinecone or
  Convex vector search) would enable accurate responses across entire books.

L-03: No Markdown Rendering in Chat
  AI responses contain markdown formatting (headers, bold, bullet points,
  code blocks) but are rendered as plain text. The react-markdown library
  was planned for integration but could not be installed in the constrained
  development environment. Chat readability suffers for structured responses.

L-04: Single-User Architecture
  Each document and chat session is scoped to a single user. There is no
  mechanism for sharing documents or collaborating on study sessions.

L-05: No Streaming Responses
  AI responses are fetched as a single blocking call. For long responses
  (quiz generation, flashcard sets), this introduces a noticeable delay
  (3-5 seconds) with no partial-result feedback to the user.

L-06: No Offline Support
  The application requires an active internet connection for all features.
  There is no caching or offline-first architecture.

L-07: File Type Support
  Only PDFs, images, and plain text files are supported. Common academic
  formats such as .docx, .pptx, .epub, and .xlsx are not handled.

8.2 Future Scope
-----------------
FS-01: Markdown Rendering
  Integrate react-markdown with remark-gfm to render AI responses as
  rich formatted text (tables, code blocks, lists, bold/italic).

FS-02: Streaming AI Responses
  Implement Server-Sent Events (SSE) or the OpenAI streaming API to
  stream AI tokens to the client incrementally, dramatically improving
  perceived response speed.

FS-03: Vector Search / Full RAG Pipeline
  Integrate Convex vector search or Pinecone to chunk, embed, and index
  document text. This would allow precise retrieval of relevant sections
  from large documents, overcoming the 8000-character context limit.

FS-04: Extended File Type Support
  Add server-side parsers for .docx (mammoth.js), .pptx (node-pptx), and
  .epub. This would make ScholarAI useful across all types of academic
  material.

FS-05: Collaborative Study Rooms
  Enable students to share documents and co-study in real-time using
  Convex's live subscription model. Shared chats and annotations could
  facilitate group study sessions.

FS-06: Spaced Repetition System (SRS)
  Persist generated flashcards and implement a spaced repetition scheduler
  (similar to Anki) to help students review cards at optimal intervals.

FS-07: Learning Analytics Dashboard
  Track study patterns: time spent per document, topics explored, quiz
  scores over time. Visualise learning progress with charts.

FS-08: Mobile Application
  Develop a React Native mobile app using Expo, sharing the same Convex
  backend, to enable on-the-go study sessions.

FS-09: OCR for Handwritten Notes
  Integrate a dedicated handwriting recognition model (e.g., Google Vision
  API) to support scanned handwritten lecture notes.

FS-10: Multi-Language Support
  Enable AI responses and UI in multiple languages to improve accessibility
  for non-English-speaking students.
